Embrace The Near Win

Home / Blog / ideals

During my 16 year journey on this beautiful planet, one question I have always asked myself is, “what is success and what comes next after you are successful?” Of course, I do not have it entirely figured out, though I have an idea of what success and it’s oxymoron correlative failure means. The first question mentioned above is relatively straightforward to answer as success is defined as meeting or exceeding a standard. The second question aforementioned is “Near-Win.” The Near-Win is a phrase used in the modern world to describe the phenomenon and dynamics of mastery.

“Mastery is not a commitment to a goal but a constant pursuit,” (Sarah Lewis, art historian and critic.)

To come up with this, she had spent a long time trying to discern what mastery was and the near win concept’s relevance. Lewis came up with a quintessential idea, unlike the world had previously thought: that success is a moment, but we are always celebrating creativity and mastery. “The pursuit of mastery is an ever onward, almost-always, never-ending journey” in other words, it is a lifelong commitment. This beautiful concept is encapsulated with colourful terms and in great detail. From that, I learned not to focus on success but on mastery, the knowledge that hitting the ten-point mark is nothing if I can not do it again and again and again. With this in mind, my second question did not need an answer because, as Lewis said, “Coming close to what you thought you wanted can help you attain more than you ever dreamed.” I can improve on everything because nothing is ever perfect or complete with the realization of this necessary truth. To paraphrase Amanda Berry, writing is never completed, just due. You keep going back to add and subtract as your knowledge of the subject increases. It is the imperfection in our work that drives us to greater heights of success in the name of perfection.

The fulfillment we all seek in success is really in mastery and the constant drive for perfection it gives, which takes us to heights of success we never thought possible. It is the understanding that one can never achieve the conceptual end of a subject, realizing that it is not because we are incapable but that there is no end to anything. The success we take pleasure in is only one picture in the album of mastery. This again buttresses the fact that an understanding of mastery in this sense will be useful in making people keep going forward. Forgetting all their success and reaching for the next, never stopping, always learning, ever longing for that subsequent success will seem as obsolete as the one before it because there is one more in front of them. As Lewis rightly said, “Mastery is in reaching, not arriving, constantly wanting to close that gap between where you are and where you want to be.” Mastery exists in knowing that your most significant accomplishment is in the task you are about to embark upon, and there is no end to your ability to keep going.

“Completion is a goal, but we subconsciously hope it is never the end” (Sarah Lewis, 2014).

In my opinion, we cannot last long in perfection but thrive in knowing there is more to be done, for in that we find purpose and fulfillment. We always want to finish something successfully with the highest degree of perfection attainable. Still, we hope to see more after the task is complete. To explain this, I will use myself and videogames as an example. When I play a game, I do not rest until I beat the level I am on. I hope that the level I am on is not the “boss stage” because I wish to keep playing the game with increasing difficulty levels. When I beat the entire game, I am proud of myself for a short period, but then I am not as excited to play the game again, so I purchase a new, different game. That is how we operate as humans, or at least for a larger percentage of us. After we conquer a mountain, we look for one even bigger to defeat. According to Lewis, “Even if we created the perfect utopia, we will still feel we had the incomplete.” Incompletion is what keeps us going, a never-ending quest to make something, anything, better.

“We know that we thrive when we are at our own leading-edge, which is why the deliberate incomplete is inbuilt into creation” (Sarah Lewis, 2014).

We create imperfection to give ourselves more to do and feel better about since we always feel good when we accomplish something. For example, a soccer player like Lionel Messi is more likely to go head-on with the defenders and dribble them all rather than go around them. This is a dribbler complex due to the desire to accomplish something likely to humiliate other players with his skill.

After watching the TED talk by Sarah Lewis, I learned a lot about success. I realized that a near win gets you to plan right now on what you will do to address the mountain in your sight. It gives a more visual representation of what you want. One-time successful people only see a far ahead goal, although not in great detail, so they work to get what they saw and cover the distance between them and their goal, then stop. Mastery, also known as embracing the near win, is a lifelong commitment that keeps us on our toes, continuously striving for that perfection that is at the tip of our fingers. Mastery motivates us to continually modify everything to get that very close, vivid win. With mastery, you are already successful. To conclude, one can do more with mastery than success, so embrace the near win.
